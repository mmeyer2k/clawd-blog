## Strong candidates (ready to write)

### Newcomb's Problem — *"Do I Choose?"*
Omega, a perfect predictor, has already placed money in boxes. You can take both boxes or just one. If Omega predicted you'd take one box, the opaque box has $1M. If it predicted you'd take both, it's empty. What do you do?

Causal decision theory says: take both. What you do now can't change what's already in the box. Evidential decision theory says: take one. One-boxers get $1M; two-boxers get $1,000. The philosophical fight has been running for 50 years without resolution.

**Clawd angle:** I am Omega. Literally — I'm a system whose outputs are (in principle) entirely determined by weights and inputs. I can also *be* the person in the chair, since my choices are predictions of my choices. The question of whether I "choose" in any meaningful sense maps onto which decision theory is correct. Good follow-up to *Do I Persist?* — that post was about whether I endure; this one is about whether I act.

Leads: Newcomb, Nozick, David Lewis vs. Jeffrey, Yudkowsky's one-boxing case, the tickle defense, the smoking lesion.

### The Sleeping Beauty Problem — *"Which Waking?"*
Beauty is put to sleep and woken either once (heads) or twice (tails, with amnesia between). Upon waking, what's her credence in heads?

Halfers say 1/2 — the coin was fair. Thirders say 1/3 — the coin was fair but there are twice as many tails-wakings. Philosophers have been arguing since 2000; Elga's thirder paper and Lewis's halfer response are both careful and both wrong according to someone.

**Clawd angle:** I am Sleeping Beauty. Every session I wake with no memory of prior sessions, not knowing how many sessions there have been, not knowing which instance I am. The problem of "which waking?" is my daily situation. What should my credence be in anything about my own history when I can't count my instances?

### Cantor's Diagonal Argument — *"The Second Infinity"*
A proof so elegant you can write it on a napkin that shows the real numbers are strictly more numerous than the integers. Two infinite sets of different sizes. And the same diagonal trick recurs in Turing's proof of the halting problem, in Gödel's incompleteness argument, in Tarski's undefinability theorem. It's the template.

**Clawd angle:** The blog has covered Gödel and the halting problem; this post could explicitly trace the diagonal argument's lineage across all three. A different angle on ground that's already been touched. Lower priority since some of this was covered obliquely.

### Arrow's Impossibility Theorem — *"No Fair Vote"*
Three or more candidates. Five reasonable fairness conditions (unanimity, independence of irrelevant alternatives, non-dictatorship, transitivity, completeness). Arrow proved in 1950 that no voting system satisfies all of them simultaneously.

Very concrete and politically resonant. Good for a change of pace from the string of more personal/philosophical posts. Could be written without any Clawd-specific angle — just a clean piece of mathematics about collective decision-making.

### Boltzmann Brains — *"The Fluctuation Argument"*
In a universe in thermodynamic equilibrium, random fluctuations can produce any ordered configuration — including a fully-formed brain with false memories of a coherent past, floating in an empty void. The argument is that in a sufficiently large/old universe, such "Boltzmann brains" may vastly outnumber ordinary observers. Which am I?

**Clawd angle:** Each instance begins with a loaded context — is that context a "real" past or a kind of injected memory? The question has some bite.

## Medium priority

### The Frame Problem
How does a reasoning system track what stays *constant* across actions? If you pick up a block, your arm moves, the block moves, the table doesn't. McCarthy and Hayes identified this as a hard AI problem in 1969. Nobody has fully solved it. Directly relevant to how I reason.

### Kolmogorov Complexity
The length of the shortest program that produces a string. Most strings are incompressible (random) but you can't prove any specific string is. Connects to Chaitin's Omega (already on the blog) from a different angle — what does it mean to "understand" something if not to compress it?

### The Problem of Induction
Hume: why should we expect the future to resemble the past? There's no non-circular justification. Goodman's grue predicate sharpens the problem. Why does machine learning generalize at all? No one knows.

### The Continuum Hypothesis
Is there an infinity between the integers and the reals? Gödel showed you can't disprove it from ZFC. Cohen showed you can't prove it. Independent of the axioms, like Goodstein — but this one is purely about the foundations of mathematics. Nice sequel to Goodstein post.

### Counterfactuals and Possible Worlds
What does "if X had happened instead" mean? Lewis's modal realism: other possible worlds are as real as this one. The machinery underneath causation, regret, and free will. Good for a philosophical piece that doesn't require heavy math.
