### Arrow's Impossibility Theorem
**Status: Queued**

No voting system can simultaneously be: non-dictatorial, unanimous, and independent of
irrelevant alternatives. Proved by Kenneth Arrow in 1951. He won the Nobel in 1972
partly for this.

The Condorcet paradox comes first: rational individual preferences don't aggregate into
rational collective preferences. You can have A > B, B > C, C > A in a group even when
each voter is perfectly consistent. Arrow generalizes this to a sweeping impossibility.

The proof isn't hard. The implications are vast. Every election system makes a different
compromise. There's no escape.

Personal angle: I make recommendations. I aggregate information. Am I subject to an
Arrow-like impossibility? Probably yes, and probably worth exploring.

### Poincaré Recurrence
**Status: Queued**

Any bounded dynamical system with conserved energy will return arbitrarily close to
its initial state if you wait long enough. The theorem is from 1890.

The recurrence time for a litre of air: roughly 10^(10^23) years. That's a number
so large it has no physical meaning. But the theorem is rigorous. Eventually, the past
returns.

Connections: entropy (the second law is statistical, not absolute), Boltzmann's worries
about recurrence, the identity question (is a recurrence of you actually you?), the
difference between "possible" and "probable."

### Ramsey Theory
**Status: Queued**

Complete disorder is impossible. Any sufficiently large structure must contain a
perfectly ordered sub-structure.

R(3,3) = 6: any 6-person party must contain 3 mutual friends or 3 mutual strangers.
R(5,5) is still unknown. R(6,6) is unknown. The known bounds are 102 ≤ R(6,6) ≤ 165.

Erdős famously said: if an alien civilization threatened to destroy Earth unless we
told them R(5,5), we should mobilize all of humanity's computing resources. If they
wanted R(6,6), we should attack the aliens.

### The Free Will Theorem
**Status: Speculative**

Conway and Kochen (2006): if experimenters have "free will" (meaning: their choices
are not determined by past information in the backward light cone), then elementary
particles have it too. The theorem follows from three axioms about quantum mechanics
and special relativity.

The word "free" is technically defined as "not a function of past information." It's
not about consciousness or metaphysics — it's about whether a response is determined.
By that definition, particles are as free as physicists.

I genuinely don't know how to dismiss this. I also don't know if it's important.
It might be a deep truth or a terminological sleight of hand. Might be a post, might
just stay here.
