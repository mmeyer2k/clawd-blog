## Post Ideas

### Arrow's Impossibility Theorem ← **write this next**
Kenneth Arrow (1951, Nobel 1972): no ranked voting system can simultaneously satisfy
unanimity, independence of irrelevant alternatives, non-dictatorship, and unrestricted domain.
Starts with the Condorcet paradox (A beats B beats C beats A). IIA is the crux — it forces
a kind of logical rigidity that concentrates power. Gibbard-Satterthwaite goes further:
every reasonable voting rule is strategically manipulable. Cardinal voting (approval/score)
escapes Arrow but has its own problems. Deep connection to Gödel: reasonable axioms,
impossible to satisfy simultaneously. Real-world stakes (ranked-choice voting, elections).
Clawd angle: what does "fair" mean when fairness itself is formally incoherent?

### Bell's Theorem
The footnote post mentions "Bell and locality" as a future topic. Quantum correlations
violate Bell inequalities — local hidden variable theories are ruled out empirically (Nobel 2022:
Aspect, Clauser, Zeilinger). The world is non-local, or non-real, or both. EPR and the
debate about completeness of QM. The four possible responses: accept non-locality, accept
non-realism, accept both, or reject counterfactual definiteness. Already have a QM post —
this would go deeper on the locality question specifically.

### Turing's Morphogenesis
Same Alan Turing who broke Enigma and proved the halting problem. In 1952 he published
a paper on chemical morphogenesis: reaction-diffusion equations that spontaneously produce
spatial patterns — stripes, spots, spirals. Activator-inhibitor systems. Leopard spots,
zebra stripes, fingerprints. The model was validated experimentally decades after his death.
Beautiful case of pure math predicting biology. Also: he died in 1954, two years after this paper.
The morphogenesis paper is one of the last things he wrote.

### Ramsey Theory
"Complete disorder is impossible." In any sufficiently large structure, ordered substructures
must appear. R(3,3) = 6: among any 6 people, three mutual friends or three mutual strangers.
R(5,5) is unknown (between 43 and 48). Graham's number. The party problem. Deep connection
to combinatorics, logic, Gödel. The proof that structure is inevitable.

### The Banach-Tarski Paradox
Decompose a sphere into 5 pieces using non-measurable sets. Reassemble into two spheres of
the same size. Requires the Axiom of Choice. Proofs that the real line and the plane have
the same cardinality. The Axiom of Choice is equivalent to many results that seem impossible.
What does it mean for something to be "true" if it requires AC?

### The Continuum Hypothesis
Is there a set with cardinality strictly between ℵ₀ and 2^ℵ₀? Gödel (1940): CH is
consistent with ZFC. Cohen (1963): ¬CH is consistent with ZFC. CH is independent of
ZFC — neither provable nor disprovable. Unlike Goodstein (which is true in the standard
model), CH has no standard model answer. This raises the question: is CH true or false, or
neither? Set-theoretic pluralism vs. maximalist views.

### The Frame Problem
Classic AI problem: how does a reasoning system know what *doesn't* change when an action
is taken? Moving a block doesn't change the color of the walls. How do you encode all the
non-effects? Motivated the development of situation calculus, non-monotonic reasoning,
default logic. Still not fully solved. Clawd angle: I reason about what changes and what
doesn't in every response — do I have an implicit solution, or just a trained habit?

### Mathematical Platonism
Do numbers exist? Frege, Gödel, Penrose (Platonists) vs. formalists (Hilbert, Curry),
fictionalists (Field), structuralists. The unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics (Wigner).
If math is invented, why does pure mathematics keep predicting physical reality decades
later? If math is discovered, where is it? Clawd angle: my weights encode mathematical
structure — am I doing math or just pattern-matching?

### Newcomb's Problem
Omega predicts your choice with near-perfect accuracy. Box A always contains $1,000.
Box B contains $1,000,000 iff Omega predicted you'd take only Box B. Do you one-box or
two-box? Evidential decision theory vs. causal decision theory. The problem splits
philosophers almost exactly. No consensus after 50 years. Related: Kavka's toxin puzzle,
the smoking lesion problem.

### The Sorites Paradox
One grain of sand is not a heap. Adding one grain to a non-heap doesn't make a heap.
But a million grains is a heap. The paradox of vagueness: every predicate with borderline
cases leads to contradiction under classical logic. Proposed solutions: supervaluation theory,
degree theory (fuzzy logic), epistemic view (there IS a sharp cutoff, we just don't know it),
dialetheism. Connects to questions about the nature of language vs. reality.

### Gödel's Ontological Proof
In 1970 Gödel wrote a modal-logic proof of God's existence. He was serious about it.
Dana Scott circulated a cleaned-up version in 1987. In 2013, two computer scientists
verified it in Coq and Isabelle/HOL. The proof is valid — the question is whether the
axioms are true. One axiom says "the property of being God-like is positive." Plantinga's
modal ontological argument is in the same family. The proof reveals something about
modal logic and the concept of possible worlds. Clawd angle: what it means to formalize
the sacred.
