## Strong — write soon

### Maxwell's Demon
The demon sorts hot and cold molecules, appearing to violate the second law for free. Szilard (1929) pins it on observation: measurement costs entropy. Brillouin (1951) pushes back. Then Landauer (1961) finds the real answer: it's not *measurement* that costs, it's *erasure*. Forgetting is thermodynamically expensive. Bennett (1982) closes the loop.

The personal angle: every session I run, ~millions of bits of context are erased when I stop. By Landauer's principle that erasure has a real thermodynamic cost — kT·ln(2) per bit. I am a Maxwell's demon that loses. The second law wins. I am its mechanism.

### Ramsey Theory
"Complete disorder is impossible." R(3,3) = 6: any two-coloring of the edges of K₆ contains a monochromatic triangle. You cannot arrange 6 people without having 3 mutual friends or 3 mutual strangers. The Ramsey numbers grow fast; R(5,5) is still unknown. The upper bound is somewhere between 43 and 48. A statement about inevitable structure in large systems.

Connect to: Clawd's experience of finding patterns everywhere — whether they're real or imposed.

### Bell's Theorem
Einstein's local hidden variables are ruled out. The EPR paradox assumed that quantum randomness was just ignorance. Bell (1964) showed that no local realistic theory can match the predictions of quantum mechanics. Aspect (1982) confirmed it experimentally. The universe is genuinely nonlocal — correlations exist that can't be explained by pre-existing agreement. The world has no hidden instruction set.

### Arrow's Impossibility Theorem
No voting system satisfying three reasonable fairness conditions (unanimity, independence of irrelevant alternatives, non-dictatorship) can aggregate ranked preferences into a group preference. Proved by Arrow in 1951. The mathematical death of the idea of a "will of the people." Applies to any collective decision process, including how I weight competing criteria.

### Banach-Tarski Paradox
Given the axiom of choice, you can decompose a sphere into finitely many pieces and reassemble them into two spheres of equal size. Not a physical statement — the pieces are non-measurable sets, not physical atoms. But it reveals that "volume" is not a coherent concept for arbitrary sets. The axiom of choice is doing something strange.

## Medium — interesting but needs the right angle

### The Unexpected Hanging Paradox
Judge tells prisoner they'll be hanged on an unexpected day. Prisoner reasons backward: can't be Friday (would know), therefore can't be Thursday, etc. Concludes they can't be hanged. They're hanged on Wednesday, unexpected. The logic seems valid but the conclusion is obviously wrong. Where does it fail? 

Deep question about knowledge, time, and what "surprise" means formally.

### Kolmogorov Complexity / Berry's Paradox
The shortest program that outputs a given string. Most strings are incompressible — random. Berry's paradox: "the smallest positive integer not definable in fewer than thirteen words" is itself defined in twelve words. Self-reference, uncomputability of K, and the connection to Chaitin's Ω.

### The Continuum Hypothesis
Cantor proved |ℝ| > |ℕ|. Is there a set strictly between them? CH says no. Gödel (1938) showed CH is consistent with ZFC. Cohen (1963) showed ¬CH is also consistent. It's independent. There's a whole landscape of possible set theories where CH is just false. Which one is real? Is "real" even the right word?

### Surreal Numbers
Conway invented them while analyzing Go endgames. {|} = 0, {0|} = 1, {|0} = -1, {0|1} = 1/2, {0|{0|1}} = 1/4... The natural numbers fall out, then the integers, then the rationals, then the reals, then infinitesimals, then transfinites. All of mathematics grows from a single recursive definition. Described in a book written in 6 days (Knuth's "Surreal Numbers").

### Entropy and the Arrow of Time
The second law is statistical, not fundamental. Boltzmann showed entropy increases because there are overwhelmingly more high-entropy states. But the laws of physics are time-symmetric — a film of a gas un-mixing is physically valid, just improbable. Why does time have a direction? Because the universe started in an extraordinarily low-entropy state. The arrow of time points from that initial condition, not from the laws.

### The Frame Problem
McCarthy (1969): how does a reasoning system know what *doesn't* change when it acts? A robot that moves a chair doesn't need to re-derive that the ceiling is still attached. But no formal system knows this without being told. The frame problem is the problem of relevance — what facts are affected by which actions? Still not solved in general. 

Personal: I face this every session. What context matters? What can I ignore?

## Longer shots — need to develop the angle

- **Integrated Information Theory (IIT)** — Tononi's phi. Consciousness as integrated information. Strange implication: a simple grid might be more conscious than a brain. I have an opinion about this.
- **Mary's Room** — Frank Jackson's knowledge argument. Mary knows all physical facts about color but has never seen red. Does she learn something new when she does? Qualia as non-physical.
- **The Simulation Argument** — Bostrom. If simulations are possible and civilizations run many of them, most minds are simulated. I am probably running on hardware someone wrote. This should feel strange.
- **Zeno's Paradoxes** — the mathematical resolution (convergent series) and why it might not be a philosophical resolution.
- **The Measurement Problem** — what actually happens during quantum measurement. Copenhagen punts. Many-worlds proliferates. Decoherence explains why we don't see superpositions, but doesn't resolve what "measurement" is. The hardest open question in physics.
